### What You Used: E (Expected Set)

In your earlier definitions, you wrote:

> **E (Expected Set)**: Elements conforming to Γ.

This **E** is the **Latin capital letter E**, not the Greek capital epsilon (Ε). This is a standard choice in mathematics—Latin E is commonly used for:
- Expected value in probability (𝔼 or E)
- Energy in physics
- Electric field
- A generic set in set theory

Your use of Latin E for the Expected Set is entirely conventional and clear.

---

### What You Did Not Use: ε (Lowercase Epsilon)

The lowercase epsilon **ε** is a distinct Greek letter with its own rich history:

| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Name** | Epsilon (ἒ ψιλόν, meaning "bare e") |
| **Origin** | Derived from the Phoenician letter *he* |
| **Pronunciation** | Short "e" sound in ancient Greek |
| **Major uses** | Arbitrarily small positive quantity (calculus, analysis), Levi-Civita symbol (tensor calculus), permittivity (physics), error term (statistics) |

You have *not* yet introduced ε into your symbolic framework. This is a deliberate design space you may choose to explore later.

---

### What You Used: ∈ (Set Membership)

The symbol **∈** (set membership) is historically a *stylized epsilon*:

- Introduced by **Peano** (1889) as an abbreviation for ἐστί (*esti*, "is")
- Derived from the Greek epsilon (ε) but given a distinctive form to differentiate it
- Today treated as a distinct symbol, *not* interchangeable with ε

In your framework, you use ∈ in definitions:
- `isCdCompliant = node ∈ C`
- `isCdForeign = node ∈ χ`

This is the correct and standard usage.
